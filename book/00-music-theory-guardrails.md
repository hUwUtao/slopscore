# Music Theory Guardrails
### The Grammar of Intentional Composition

> **Status:** Normative. All rules apply unless the user brief explicitly overrides them.  
> **Scope:** Melody · Harmony · Phrase · Cadence · Voice leading · NCTs · Rhythm

---

## 1. Phrase and Form

- Build with **4-bar or 8-bar phrases** unless the brief specifies otherwise.
- Prefer one of two proven phrase archetypes:

| Archetype | Structure |
|---|---|
| **Sentence** | Present a basic idea → repeat or restate it → continue and cadence |
| **Period** | Antecedent phrase ending weakly → consequent phrase ending more strongly |

- Make phrase endings progressively stronger across the section.
- **Typical safe section map:**

| Bars | Function |
|---|---|
| 1–4 | Establish motive and tonic area |
| 5–8 | Depart, intensify, cadence |
| 9–12 | Vary or sequence earlier material |
| 13–16 | Dominant preparation and strongest close |

---

## 2. Melody

- Derive most melodic content from **one or two short motives**.
- Repeat motives with variation by any of these operations:
  - **Sequence** — repeat at a different pitch level
  - **Rhythmic displacement** — shift the motive's metric position
  - **Interval expansion or compression** — widen or narrow the intervals
  - **Inversion fragments** — flip the contour
  - **Truncation or extension** — shorten or lengthen the cell
- Place **chord tones on structurally strong beats**: bar 1, bar 3 / midpoint, cadential bars.
- **Default to stepwise motion**; treat leaps as special events requiring preparation or recovery.
- After a large leap, restore balance with stepwise motion in the **opposite direction**.
- Aim for **one clear apex note** per phrase or section — avoid constant peak-chasing.
- Use repetition deliberately; do not constantly change contour without motive recall.

## 3. Harmony

### 3.1 Harmonic Function

- Make harmonic function legible at every moment:

| Function | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Tonic** (I / i) | Stability, arrival, home |
| **Predominant** (IV, ii, etc.) | Departure, preparation, momentum |
| **Dominant** (V, V7) | Tension demanding resolution |

- Harmonic rhythm should **support phrasing**:
  - Faster harmonic change → greater tension
  - Slower harmonic change → stability or suspension of time

### 3.2 Safe Tonal Progressions

| Mode | Progressions |
|---|---|
| Major | I–IV–V–I · I–vi–IV–V · ii–V–I |
| Minor | i–iv–V–i · i–VI–III–VII · i–iv–VII–III–VI–ii°–V–i |

- Use applied dominants or tonicization **sparingly** so the home key remains audible.
- Avoid chord changes that feel arbitrary or disconnected from melodic accents.

### 3.3 Chord Economy (Voicing)

When voicing is sparse:
- The **fifth** is the first note removable.
- The **third** defines mode — keep it.
- The **seventh** defines dominant function — keep it.
- Double the **root** first, then the fifth. Avoid gratuitous third doublings.

## 4. Cadences

Use cadences **intentionally**, not accidentally.

| Cadence | Formula | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Perfect Authentic (PAC)** | V–I, both in root position, melody ends on scale degree 1 | Strongest closure |
| **Imperfect Authentic (IAC)** | V–I, but inverted or melody does not end on scale degree 1 | Medium closure |
| **Half Cadence (HC)** | Any chord → V | Open, questioning, incomplete |
| **Deceptive** | V–vi or V–VI | Delays closure, redirects energy |
| **Plagal** | IV–I | Soft, devotional reinforcement (often post-cadential) |

### Cadence Placement Rules

- **Antecedent phrases** usually end with HC or IAC.
- **Consequent phrases** usually end with PAC.
- Save the **most definitive cadence** for the last important phrase of the section.
- Use a **weaker cadence mid-section** and the strongest at the end.
- Do not place full cadences on upbeats unless the style specifically calls for it.

## 5. Voice Leading

- Think **horizontally** (each line as a melody) before thinking vertically (chords).
- **Preferred order of motion across chord changes:**
  1. Common tones (hold the shared note)
  2. Semitone or whole-step motion
  3. Small skips (third, fourth)
  4. Large leaps only when stylistically necessary

- **Leading tone (`ti`)** should normally resolve to tonic (`do`) in the same voice;  
  do not double the leading tone.
- Let **bass motion define large harmonic movement** while inner parts move smoothly.
- Avoid simultaneous large leaps in all voices — stagger motion or use contrary motion.
- In minor keys, raise the **6th and 7th scale degrees** in ascending lines (melodic minor);  
  lower them in descending lines.

### Natural Register Order (Woodwinds — Rimsky-Korsakov)

> Piccolo → Flute → Oboe → Clarinet → Bassoon

Inverting this order — e.g. placing Bassoon above Clarinet — creates an "unnatural tone."  
Only break this order when the compositional intent is estrangement or special color.

## 6. Non-Chord Tones (NCTs) and Embellishment

| NCT Type | How It Works | Usage Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Passing tone** | Fills a third by step between two stable tones | Usually on a weak beat or weak part of beat |
| **Neighbor tone** | Leaves a stable note by step and returns to it | Upper or lower; create gentle oscillation |
| **Suspension** | Holds a note over a chord change, then resolves downward by step | Creates longing; resolve on a weak beat |
| **Anticipation** | Arrives early before the chord it belongs to | Common in cadential approach |
| **Escape tone** | Leaves a stable note by step, then leaps away | Decorative; not structural |
| **Appoggiatura** | Approached by leap, resolved by step | Emotionally expressive; use sparingly |
| **Pedal point** | Sustained bass note while harmony changes above | Tonic or dominant pedals are most stable |

- Put the most structurally important chord tones on **strong beats**.
- Place embellishing tones around them on weak beats or weak metric positions.
- If a line contains many NCTs, the underlying chord **skeleton must still sing clearly**.

## 7. Rhythm

- Establish a **clear rhythmic cell early** — the "motor" of the piece.
- Vary density **across phrases** rather than using constant note values throughout:
  - Opening phrases: often simpler, clearer rhythm
  - Middle / developmental passages: denser, more active
  - Structural arrivals and cadences: rhythmically clearer for emphasis
- **Syncopation** should reinforce style and energy, not obscure where the beat lands.
- Strong cadential bars benefit from **clearer rhythmic articulation** (avoid syncopation landing on the resolution).
- If groove is percussion-driven, make melodic rhythm **lock with or intentionally push against** the grid — not float ambiguously.

### Meter-Specific Notes

| Meter | Natural stress | Notes for writing |
|---|---|---|
| 4/4 | Beats 1 and 3 strong | Most flexible; avoid making beats 2 and 4 feel stronger than 1 and 3 unless syncopation is intentional |
| 3/4 | Beat 1 strong | Waltz feel; phrase naturally ends on beat 1 after a 4- or 8-bar group |
| 6/8 | Beats 1 and 4 (compound) | Think in two large beats, not six small ones |
| 5/4 | Often 3+2 or 2+3 | Commit to one grouping per phrase; maintain it or make the shift purposeful |
| 7/8 | Often 2+2+3 or 3+2+2 | The "extra" eighth note usually falls at the end of the bar |

## 8. Mode Reference

| Mode | Pattern from root | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Ionian (Major) | W W H W W W H | Bright, resolved, familiar |
| Dorian | W H W W W H W | Minor with raised 6th; melancholic yet mobile |
| Phrygian | H W W W H W W | Dark, Spanish, tense half-step above root |
| Lydian | W W W H W W H | Dreamy, bright, floating (#4) |
| Mixolydian | W W H W W H W | Major with lowered 7th; folksy, incomplete-feeling |
| Aeolian (Natural Minor) | W H W W H W W | Sad, stable minor |
| Locrian | H W W H W W W | Extremely unstable; tritone above root; avoid as home key |

> W = whole step, H = half step
